Adams’ ‘City of Yes for Economic Opportunity’ plan faces criticism at public hearing

By Tsehai Alfred. Graphics by Diya Nair.

January 23, 2024

Grace Li / Senior Staff Photographer

At a public hearing hosted by Community Board 9 and the Department of City Planning on Thursday, community members provided feedback on “City of Yes for Economic Opportunity,” a text amendment aimed at creating more flexible zoning regulations for small businesses. While the plan impacts zoning regulations across New York City, CB9 board members specifically expressed concerns of an increase in the real estate shortage and residential disturbance.

“City of Yes for Economic Opportunity” is one of three initiatives created by Mayor Eric Adams to bolster sustainability, small businesses, and affordable housing through updates to zoning. The initiative“City of Yes for Economic Opportunity” outlines 18 proposals under four goals: expand options for businesses to locate; support growing industries; foster vibrant neighborhoods; and increase opportunities for business growth with proposals like allowing lifetime limits to reactivate vacant storefronts.

The first initiative on sustainability, “City of Yes for Carbon Neutrality,” was passed by City Council on Dec. 6, 2023, and “City of Yes for Economic Opportunity” is currently in a public review process that began on Oct. 30, 2023, and includes public hearings from every community board. The third and final initiative, “City of Yes for Housing Opportunity,” will enter public review in the spring. As part of this public review process, community members and CB9 members gave recommendations to amend the proposal, following a brief presentation of the plan by a Department of City Planning representative.

“Our zoning rules that regulate business haven’t been updated since JFK was the president,” Audrey Wachs, the Department of City Planning representative and urban planner working with CB9, said. “So the current zoning often places outdated and sometimes counterproductive regulations on business activities.”

New York City’s current commercial zoning regulations originate from the 1961 Zoning Resolution, which created eight commercial districts with different use standards. For example, the commercial 1st and 2nd commercial dDistricts are designated for small retail stores that serve the surrounding residential community. The Adams administration claims that these zoning rules constrain the operation of businesses in commercial districts and confuse businesses with ambiguous regulations.

However, community members worry that the proposal, which expands the overlap of commercial and residential districts, will exacerbate the shortage of residential real estate and affordable housing in West Harlem, Manhattanville, and Hamilton Heights. The area is already one of the most densely developed districts in the city.

“CB9 believes that the creation of upper-floor retail, expanded storefronts, and corner bodegas will create an increased strain on existing real estate in the form of greater demand for limited square footage, raising prices,” Barry Weinberg, first vice chair of CB9, said at the public hearing, quoting from CB9’s recommendations to the proposal.

Lydia Gerson, a CB9 member, echoed Weinberg’s sentiments in her testimonial statement to the Department of City Planning representatives, receiving applause from other public hearing attendees.

“I don’t see this as anything but giving a big ‘yes’ to big box, big real estate in the city, which have made life intolerable here for many of us for the last 25 years,” Gerson said.

CB9 board members also expressed concern over proposal nine, which aims to support and expand nightlife enterprises through “common sense” rules for dancing and live entertainment. As most of the commercial spaces in CB9 are on the ground floor of residential buildings, community members worry that potential side effects of nightlife, like noise pollution and violence, will disturb residents.

Victor Edwards, chair of CB9, said to the city planning representatives that residents are concerned about noise pollution because measures like calling 311, the number that responds to noise complaints, are already unreliable.

“We find that in the community at large, we don’t get much follow- up in 311,” Edwards said at the hearing. “Now, if you put commercial establishments in a residential building, people are concerned about how that will be enforced.”

Following testimonials and questions, 27 community board members voted in favor of CB9’s recommendations to the plan, two opposed, and one abstained. Of the public members, one voted in favor, one opposed, and one abstained.

Community boards have until Jan. 31 to make recommendations to the plan, and online comments from CB9 residents are being accepted until Jan. 24. The City Planning Commission will hold their own public hearing on the plan on Jan. 23.
